# Every number, in plain English

Plain-English tutorials for the numbers in Underlisted. Each = one-line definition,
a real-dollar example, and "why it matters to you." Wording kept consistent with
`src/glossary.py` and `site/learn.html` (no drift). The HTML twin is the deliverable:
`content/investor-terms-explained.html`.

> Honest note: every number is an ESTIMATE — a screening tool, not financial/legal advice.

---

## Buyer terms (already live — wording matches the glossary)

### Deal Score (0–100)
One simple number that tells you, at a glance, how good a home looks as a deal. Higher is better.
Green = good deal · amber = look closer · red = probably not great. (70–100 good, 45–69 okay, 0–44 weak.)
**Why it matters:** scan green dots instead of spreadsheets. A first filter, not the final word.

### Can I Afford It? (green / amber / red)
You tell us income, savings and monthly debts; we check whether a home fits YOUR money.
Green = comfortably yes · amber = tight · red = a stretch. Shows what you'd have left each month.
*Example:* "Green — about $600 left every month after the home is paid for." Numbers stay private.
**Why it matters:** a great deal can still be wrong for your budget. This answers "can I carry it?"

### True monthly cost (PITI)
The real monthly number to own a home, not just the loan. PITI = Principal + Interest + Tax + Insurance,
plus PMI/HOA (if any) and a little upkeep.
*Example:* loan alone $1,500/mo, but really ~$2,050/mo with everything.
**Why it matters:** sites show only the loan; the real bills surprise first-time buyers.

### Cash to close & down payment
The real cash needed up front, before move-in: down payment + closing costs.
*Example:* $300k home, 10% down = $30,000 + a few thousand closing = ~$35,000 in hand.
**Why it matters:** tells you if you can buy now or need to save more. The #1 surprise.

### PMI (private mortgage insurance)
Extra monthly fee when your down payment is under 20%. Protects the lender; goes away later.
*Example:* 10% down → maybe $100–$200/mo PMI until you build more ownership.
**Why it matters:** lets you buy sooner but quietly raises your true monthly cost.

## Investor terms (the new Investor Tools page)

### Rental cash flow
Money left in your pocket each month after EVERY cost. Rent minus mortgage minus running costs
(tax, insurance, HOA, upkeep) minus vacancy and management.
*Example:* rent $2,000 − mortgage ~$1,400 − costs ~$450 = ~$150/mo. Shown as an honest range.
**Why it matters:** cash flow is what you live on. Green only when there's a real cushion (~$100/mo+).

### Cap rate
The yearly return from rent, BEFORE any mortgage, as a % of price. NOI ÷ price.
*Example:* $200k home nets $12,000/yr → 6% cap rate.
**Why it matters:** ignores the loan, so it compares homes fairly — "is the rent strong for this price?"

### Cash-on-cash return
Yearly cash flow ÷ the cash you put in (down payment + closing). Includes the loan (cap rate doesn't).
*Example:* $40,000 in, $150/mo = $1,800/yr → 4.5% cash-on-cash.
**Why it matters:** the return on YOUR own money vs. leaving it in the bank.

### The 1% rule
A 5-second check: monthly rent should be ≥ 1% of the price. A rough rule of thumb, not a law.
*Example:* $200k home needs ~$2,000/mo to pass; $1,500 fails (a yellow flag, common in pricier areas).
**Why it matters:** reject weak deals fast, then trust the real cash-flow math.

### After-Repair Value (ARV)
What a home will likely be worth AFTER you fix it up, based on renovated comparable sales nearby.
*Example:* buy at $150k, spend $40k; renovated homes sell ~$250k → ARV $250,000.
**Why it matters:** the finish line for a flip. Guess too high and "profit" vanishes. Use SOLD comps.

### The fix-&-flip 70% rule
Don't pay more than 70% of ARV, minus repairs. The 30% gap covers holding, selling, surprises + profit.
Max offer = (ARV × 70%) − repairs.
*Example:* ARV $250k, repairs $40k → (175,000) − 40,000 = $135,000 max offer.
**Why it matters:** flips go wrong by overpaying at the start. Our tool flags amber if you go over.

### Holding costs & selling costs (in a flip)
Holding = taxes/insurance/utilities/loan interest while you own it. Selling = agent commission + closing.
*Example:* hold $150k home 5 months ≈ $3,750; sell $250k home ≈ $20,000 (~8%). Together ~$24,000 off profit.
**Why it matters:** ignore these and a flip looks far rosier than it is. Speed matters — holding eats profit.
