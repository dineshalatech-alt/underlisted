If you're buying a home to live in

🎯Deal Score (0–100)

One simple number that tells you, at a glance, how good a home looks as a deal. Higher is better.

We color it so you don't have to think: green = looks like a good deal, amber = okay, look closer, red = probably not a great deal.

70–100 Good 45–69 Okay 0–44 Weak

Why it matters to you: instead of comparing ten spreadsheets, you scan green dots. It's a fast first filter, not the final word — always read the details on a home you like.

✅Can I Afford It? (green / amber / red)

You tell us your income, savings and monthly debts. We check whether a home fits your money — and answer in plain colors.

Green = comfortably yes. Amber = tight, be careful. Red = a stretch right now. We also show how much you'd have left over each month.

Example: "Green — you'd have about $600 left every month after the home is paid for." Your numbers stay private.

Why it matters to you: a home can be a great deal and still be wrong for your budget. This answers the question the deal score can't: can I actually carry this?

💸True monthly cost (PITI)

The real number you pay each month to own a home — not just the loan payment.

Lenders call the core of it PITI: Principal + Interest (the loan) + property Tax + Insurance. We add PMI (if any), HOA (if any) and a little for upkeep on top, so the number matches real life.

Example: the loan alone is $1,500/mo, but with tax, insurance and the rest it's really about $2,050/mo. That $2,050 is the number you actually pay.

Why it matters to you: most websites show only the loan and the home feels affordable — until the real bills arrive. We show the full number up front so there's no nasty surprise.

💵Cash to close & down payment

The real cash you need in hand up front to buy the home — before you move in.

It's mainly your down payment (your share of the price) plus closing costs (one-time fees for paperwork, the loan and so on).

Example: on a $300,000 home, a 10% down payment is $30,000, plus a few thousand in closing costs — so you'd need roughly $35,000 in hand.

Why it matters to you: this is the number that tells you if you can buy now or need to save a bit more. It's the most common surprise for first-time buyers.

🛡️PMI (private mortgage insurance)

An extra monthly fee you pay when your down payment is under 20%. It protects the lender, not you.

The good news: once you've paid down enough of the loan, PMI usually goes away.

Example: you put down 10% instead of 20%, so you pay maybe $100–$200 a month extra in PMI until you build up more ownership.

Why it matters to you: a smaller down payment lets you buy sooner, but PMI quietly raises your true monthly cost. Knowing it's there helps you weigh "buy now" against "save more first."

💰Rental cash flow

The money left in your pocket each month after every cost is paid.

It's the rent, minus the mortgage, minus the running costs: tax, insurance, HOA, upkeep, plus a slice for empty months (vacancy) and a manager if you hire one. What's left is your cash flow. It can be positive (you earn) or negative (you pay to hold it).

Example: rent is $2,000/mo. Mortgage is about $1,400 and all the running costs are about $450, so roughly $150/mo lands in your pocket. We show it as an honest range, not one rosy number.

Why it matters to you: cash flow is what you actually live on. A property that "looks cheap" but loses money every month is a bill, not an investment. We mark it green only when there's a real cushion (about $100/mo or more).

📊Cap rate (capitalization rate)

The yearly return a property makes from its rent, before any mortgage — as a percentage of its price.

Take the rent for a year, subtract the running costs (but not the loan). That's the yearly profit, called NOI (net operating income). Divide it by the price. Higher usually means a stronger income property.

Example: a $200,000 home nets $12,000 a year after running costs. $12,000 ÷ $200,000 = a 6% cap rate.

Why it matters to you: because it ignores the loan, cap rate lets you compare two homes fairly — like comparing the engines, not the financing. It's the quickest way to ask "is the rent strong for this price?"

💲Cash-on-cash return

How hard the actual cash you put in is working — your yearly cash flow as a percentage of the cash you spent up front.

Cap rate ignores the loan; cash-on-cash includes it. Take a full year of cash flow and divide it by your cash to close (down payment + closing costs).

Example: you put $40,000 down and the home throws off $150/mo — that's $1,800 a year. $1,800 ÷ $40,000 = a 4.5% cash-on-cash return.

Why it matters to you: this is the return on your own money. It tells you whether the cash would do better in this house than sitting in the bank — the real test for a beginner investor.

✅The 1% rule

A 5-second sanity check: monthly rent should be at least 1% of the home's price.

It's a rough rule of thumb, not a law. Pass it and the deal is worth a closer look. Fail it and the cash flow is often thin — common in pricier areas, so don't panic, just dig deeper.

Example: a $200,000 home would need about $2,000/mo rent to pass. Rent of $1,500 fails the rule — a yellow flag, not a hard no.

Why it matters to you: it lets you reject obviously weak deals in seconds before running full numbers. Treat it as a filter, then trust the real cash-flow math.

🏗️After-Repair Value (ARV)

What a home will likely be worth after you fix it up — not what it's worth as a fixer right now.

You estimate ARV from what similar, already-renovated homes nearby have sold for. Every flip number depends on getting this right.

Example: you buy a tired house for $150,000 and spend $40,000 fixing it. Renovated homes like it sell for around $250,000 — so the ARV is $250,000.

Why it matters to you: ARV is the finish line for a flip. Guess it too high and a "profit" can vanish. Keep it realistic and check it against recent sold homes, not hopeful listings.

🔢The fix-&-flip 70% rule

Don't pay more than 70% of the after-repair value, minus your repair budget.

That 30% gap is your cushion — it has to cover holding costs, selling costs, surprises, and your profit. The formula: max offer = (ARV × 70%) − repairs.

Example: ARV is $250,000, repairs are $40,000. 70% of $250,000 = $175,000, minus $40,000 = a $135,000 max offer. Pay more and your safety cushion shrinks.

Why it matters to you: flips go wrong when you overpay at the start. This rule sets a ceiling that keeps room for profit when (not if) something costs more than planned. Our tool flags amber when you go over it.

💰Holding costs & selling costs (in a flip)

The two cost buckets beginners forget — what you pay while you own it, and what you pay when you sell it.

Holding costs: taxes, insurance, utilities and loan interest for every month you own the house. Selling costs: the agent's commission and closing fees when you finally sell — usually a big chunk of the sale price.

Example: holding a $150,000 home for 5 months might cost about $3,750 (taxes, insurance, interest). Selling a $250,000 home can cost about $20,000 (around 8% in commission and fees). Together that's nearly $24,000 off your profit.

Why it matters to you: ignore these and a flip looks far more profitable than it is. The longer a project drags on, the more holding costs eat your profit — so speed matters. Our flip tool includes both automatically.
